Unit 2: Making Sense of Information Organization

Key Learnings

Unit 2 focused on how information is structured to make it findable and usable. We covered metadata, classification systems (like taxonomies and folksonomies), and the principles behind organizing digital and physical information. I learned that good organization is invisible—when it works, we don’t notice it; when it fails, chaos ensues.

My Reflection

The concept of metadata fascinated me. I never realized that every photo I take contains hidden data—date, time, location, camera settings—that makes it searchable. This clicked when I thought about how I find old photos by searching “beach” or “2025”. The computer isn’t really understanding the image; it’s using the metadata I (or my phone) attached.

Another eye-opener was the difference between taxonomies (like library classification) and folksonomies (like hashtags on Instagram). Taxonomies are structured and controlled, while folksonomies emerge from users. I see this in how I use folders vs. tags to organize my files. Folders force a single category, but tags let me assign multiple labels—much more flexible.

Challenges

I struggled with understanding how these principles apply to large-scale systems like Google or Netflix. How do they organize billions of items and still return relevant results? The idea of algorithms using metadata and user behavior to rank results helped, but I still have questions about bias and fairness.

Real-World Connection

This unit made me critically examine apps I use daily. Spotify’s playlists, for example, are organized by genre, mood, and user listening history. It’s a combination of human-curated taxonomies and algorithmic folksonomies. Understanding this changes how I see technology—it’s not magic, it’s design.

Questions I’m Pondering

- How do we ensure metadata isn’t used to manipulate or discriminate?

- Can a single organization system work for everyone, or do we need personalized approaches?

- What happens when information is mislabeled or lacks metadata.
